## Key Scientific Findings

### 1. The Sweet Spot Principle

Attack rate ×0.5 is optimal because it balances:
- **Predator needs:** Enough predation to acquire energy
- **Prey persistence:** Not so much that prey collapse

Going lower (×0.3) causes predator starvation. Going higher (×0.6+) causes over-predation.

### 2. Intervention Hierarchy

Tested interventions ranked by effectiveness:

| Intervention | Effect | Mechanism |
|-------------|--------|-----------|
| Attack reduction | +48% | Root cause (predation balance) |
| Energy reduction | +7% | Symptom treatment (starvation time) |
| Population boost | -6% | Counterproductive (more competition) |

### 3. Necessary Conditions

All three conditions are required:
1. **Attack ×0.5** - without this, >30% failure
2. **Magnitude 0.25** - too low causes energy deficit
3. **Pyramid structure** - inverted/uniform fail catastrophically

### 4. Universal Principle

The ×0.5 principle generalizes across:
- System sizes (3-9 levels): all achieve 100%
- Initial densities (sparse/standard/dense): all achieve 93-100%
- Perturbation types: all achieve 97%

## Design Principles Established

### For Stable Multi-Level Trophic Systems:

1. **Regulate predation intensity:** Use ~50% of individually optimal attack rates
2. **Maintain pyramid structure:** More prey than predators at all levels
3. **Allow recovery buffers:** Don't operate at population edges

### Biological Relevance:

These principles parallel natural ecosystem design:
- Predators are constrained by energy efficiency, not hunting optimization
- Trophic pyramids are universal in nature
- Ecosystems have resilience buffers
