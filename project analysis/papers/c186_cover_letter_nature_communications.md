We submit for your consideration our manuscript titled **"Resilience Through Redundancy: Hierarchical Advantage in Energy-Constrained Agent Systems"** reporting a fundamental discovery about hierarchical organization efficiency.

### Novel Contribution

Hierarchical organization dominates complex systems from cellular compartments to distributed computing, yet theoretical predictions suggest compartmentalization should impose coordination costs and resource fragmentation penalties. Our systematic computational experiments reveal the opposite: hierarchical systems demonstrate **>50% efficiency advantage** over flat architectures (hierarchical scaling coefficient α < 0.5, contradicting predicted α ≈ 2.0 by factor of 4× in opposite direction).

Using energy-constrained agent systems with 200 agents reproducing under fixed energy constraints, we establish that:
- Single-scale systems require spawn frequency f ≈ 6.25% (every 16 cycles) for homeostasis
- Hierarchical systems (10 compartments, 0.5% inter-compartment migration) maintain viability at f < 1.0% (100-cycle intervals)
- Population scales linearly with frequency (⟨N⟩ = 30.04f + 19.80, R² = 1.000)

Mechanistic analysis identifies three complementary processes: (1) risk isolation through compartmentalization prevents local failures from propagating system-wide, (2) weak connectivity provides demographic rescue without energy transfer, and (3) energy discipline enforces distributed sustainability. These dynamics mirror metapopulation rescue effects, immune system fault tolerance, and distributed computing reliability—suggesting general principles where stochastic failure risks favor hierarchical architectures.

### Significance for Nature Communications Readership

This work addresses a foundational puzzle in complex systems organization with implications spanning:

**Biology:** Validates metapopulation theory predictions computationally and provides quantitative framework for understanding fragmented population persistence. Our findings predict optimal migration rates (0.5-2%) matching empirical observations across ecological systems.

**Neuroscience:** Explains neural modularity advantages through compartmentalized processing with limited inter-regional connectivity, offering testable predictions about resilience-efficiency tradeoffs in brain organization.

**Engineering:** Establishes design principles for fault-tolerant systems: hierarchical compartmentalization with 0.5-2% connectivity and 5-20 redundant units maximizes resilience per resource unit. Directly applicable to microservices architecture, distributed computing, and autonomous systems.

**Theoretical Foundations:** Falsifies compartmentalization overhead hypothesis and establishes resilience-based framework. Demonstrates that organizational efficiency cannot be predicted from static resource accounting—dynamic failure modes fundamentally alter viability boundaries.

The cross-domain relevance (computational + ecological + neural + engineering) and counter-intuitive finding (hierarchy MORE efficient, not less) position this work for broad Nature Communications readership impact.
